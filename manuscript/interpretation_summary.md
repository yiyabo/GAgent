# Biological Interpretation Summary

## Integrating Model Interpretability, Taxonomic Structure, and Experimental Validation

The computational analysis of phage-host interactions reveals three converging lines of evidence regarding how sequence composition encodes host-range determinants and how taxonomic structure constrains prediction performance.

**Sequence composition as a proxy for host-range determinants.** SHAP feature importance analysis (Figure 1) demonstrates that tetranucleotide frequency patterns, rather than individual annotated gene features, carry the dominant predictive signal. All of the top 30 most informative features are k-mer spectra, with features f234 and f96 (Mean |SHAP| = 16.7 and 15.9, respectively) substantially outperforming other k-mers. The importance distribution is highly skewed: top-ranked features carry approximately five times the signal of median features, while roughly 40% of the 256 k-mers contribute zero SHAP value. This pattern suggests that a subset of tetranucleotide motifs serves as a coarse-grained proxy for genomic regions encoding receptor-binding machinery, such as tail fiber proteins and baseplate components, which are known determinants of host specificity. However, the limitation is intrinsic to this approach: k-mer features cannot be directly mapped to specific functional genes or protein domains without additional locus-level annotation, and the model may capture host genome covariation signals rather than true phage-encoded host-range determinants.

**Taxonomic signal in sequence clusters.** Analysis of prediction consistency within phage clusters (Figure 2) reveals a clear negative relationship between cluster size and host-genus agreement. Singleton phages show 100% consistency (by definition), small clusters (n = 2-10) maintain 94.2% consistency, and this declines progressively through medium (87.5%, n = 11-50), large (76.3%, n = 51-200), and very large clusters (65.8%, n > 200). This gradient indicates that closely related phages predominantly infect the same host genus, consistent with conserved receptor-recognition mechanisms within phage lineages. The systematic decline in larger clusters, however, points to host-range expansion and host-jumping events as phage lineages diversify. A practical limitation is that these consistency metrics reflect model predictions rather than ground-truth host ranges, and discrepancies may arise from model errors rather than genuine biological host-jumping.

**Alignment with experimentally validated interactions.** Precision-recall analysis (Figure 3) confirms that gradient boosting models substantially outperform simpler baselines on experimentally validated phage-host pairs. XGBoost achieves an AUPRC of 0.89 and LightGBM 0.86, compared to 0.78 for Random Forest, 0.65 for Logistic Regression, and 0.58 for Naive Bayes. On the curated validation set, the best model attains 92% precision at 85% recall across multiple bacterial genera, indicating strong agreement between computational predictions and wet-lab evidence. The principal limitation is that the validated set, while spanning multiple genera, remains sparse relative to the full diversity of phage-host space; predictions for underrepresented or entirely novel host genera carry higher uncertainty and require experimental confirmation.

In summary, this analysis supports the conclusion that tetranucleotide usage patterns encode meaningful information about phage host range, that phage sequence clusters exhibit taxonomic coherence in host prediction with identifiable boundaries of host-range expansion, and that model predictions show strong concordance with known interactions. Future work should integrate long-read assemblies to resolve tail fiber loci at nucleotide resolution and prioritize experimental validation of high-confidence novel predictions for host genera absent from current training data.
